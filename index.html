Medications

The success of ivf largely depends on growing multiple eggs at once. Injections of the natural hormones fsh and/or lh (gonadotropins) are used for this purpose. Additional medications are used to prevent premature ovulation. An overly vigorous response can occur, or conversely an inadequate response

Transvaginal Oocyte Retrieval

When the eggs are ready to be removed based on your blood work and ultrasound,

The eggs are removed from the ovary through the vagina with a needle under ultrasound guidance.

Anesthesia is provided to make this comfortable. The procedure takes no longer than 15 minutes and within 30 to 60 minutes, patients are ready to go home.

Injury and infection are rare risks associated with this procedure.

After a few days of development, the best appearing embryos are selected for your embryo transfer.

By Day 3, the Second cell division (6-8 cell embryos) occurs and ideally we want to see 6 to 8 cell embryos that are round and clear. The embryologist assess the embryos on this day to determine if an embryo transfer should occur or if we can grow the embryos to day 5 or 6 that allows better embryo selection and in hopes of transferring less embryos.

The embryo transfer is performed in a procedure room between 2 and 6 days following your egg retrieval. This is tailored to your clinical situation and the quality of your embryos. Our goal is to optimize your chances of conception, while at the same time minimizing your chances of having a multiple pregnancy. These are taken from the embryo culture into an embryo transfer catheter by our embryologist and are gently introduced into your uterine cavity similar to having a Pap smear.

The embryo transfer is performed using ultrasound guidance to assure your embryos are in the correct place within your uterus.

Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection (ICSI)

Indications:

ICSI is used to increase the chance of fertilization when fertilization rate is expected to be lower than normal due to male factor infertility. Often there are abnormal semen characteristics, including low sperm counts, reduced motility (movement) or abnormal morphology (shapes).


Male factor infertility can be due to several factors, including:


  1. 1. Anatomic such as varicoceles (engorged blood vessels in the scrotum and testicles)
  2. 2. Obstructive Outflow problems such as previous vasectomy or congenital bilateral absence of vas deferens (CBAVD) CBAVD is often associated with Cystic Fibrosis and is due to failure of the tubes connecting the testes to the penis to develop correctly.
  3. 3. Non-obstructive azoospermia such as hormonal problems or failure of the testes to produce sperm resulting in a lack of sperm in the ejaculate.

Both IVF and ICSI can bypass male factor issues. When there is no sperm in the ejaclate, sperm can often aspirated directly from the testicles or epididymis to achieve fertilization.

Procedure:

ICSI is the direct injection of a single sperm into the egg using an extremely thin needle.

Birth Defect Risks:

The absolute risk is very low including injury to the egg and some reports suggest a small increase risk of birth defects. However, the risk has mainly been attributed to infertility factors rather than the procedure itself.


It should be noted that ICSI will not improve an egg defect.

Genetic Related Risks:

An increased risk of genetic defects in offspring has been reported. The prevalence of chromosomal abnormalities has been reported to be slightly higher in children conceived via ICSI (0.8-1.0%) compared to children undergoing IVF without ICSI (0.2%). While the risk is still extremely low, the higher rate appears to reflect a male issue and not the procedure itself. Since men with CBAVD are affected with a mild form of cystic fibrosis, it is possible that this gene defect may be passed on to their offspring.

Embryo Disposition

Freezing of viable embryos not transferred after egg retrieval provides additional changes for pregnancy. Frozen embryos do not always survive the process of freezing and thawing. Currently, freezing eggs before fertilization is less successful than freezing fertilized eggs (embryos). Ethical and legal dilemmas can arise when couples separate or divorce; therefore, disposition agreements are essential. It is the responsibility of each couple with frozen embryos to remain in contact with ARI on a quarterly basis.

Cryopreservation:

Indications:

Following IVF, remaining oocytes or embryos may be frozen (cryopreserved) for future use.

Summary of indications:

  • To reduce the risks of multiple gestation
  • To preserve fertility potential in the face of certain necessary medical procedures.
  • To increase the chance of having one or more pregnancies from a single cycle of ovarian stimulation.
  • To minimize the medical risk and cost to the patient by decreasing the number of stimulated cycles and egg retrievals.
  • To temporarily delay pregnancy and risk of Ovarian Hyperstimulation Syndrome

Risks:

Overall pregnancy rates at the national level with frozen embryos are lower than pregnancy rates with fresh embryos.

Data does not indicate any likelihood that children born of embryos that have been cryopreserved and thawed will experience greater risk of abnormalities than those born of fresh embryos.

Legal Considerations:

Because of the possibility of you and/or your partner’s separation, death or incapacitation, it is important to decide on the disposition of any embryo(s), fresh or cryopreserved, that remain in the laboratory. Since this is a rapidly evolving field, both medically and legally, ARI cannot guarantee what the available or acceptable avenues for disposition will be at any future date

Alternatives:

Discarding the cryopreserved embryo(s)

Donating the cryopreserved embryo(s) for approved research studies

Donating the cryopreserved embryos to another couple in order to attempt pregnancy (You may be asked to undergo additional infectious disease testing and screening recommended by the FDA if you select this option.)

Various circumstances:

Embryos are understood to be your property, with rights of survivorship. No use can be made of these embryos without the consent of both partners (if applicable):

  • In the event of divorce or dissolution of the marriage or partnership, the ownership and/or other rights to the embryo(s) will be as directed by the court decree and/or settlement agreement.
  • In the event of the death or incapacitation of one partner, the embryo(s) will become the sole and exclusive property of the surviving partner.
  • In the event of death or incapacitation of both partners or of a last surviving partner, the embryo(s) shall become the sole and exclusive property of ARI. Your decision in this event is documented in the consent form.

Risks to the Woman

Ovarian Hyper-stimulation Syndrome:

OHSS is the most serious side effect of fertility medications (ovarian stimulation).


Signs and Symptoms:

  • Increased ovarian size
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Accumulation of fluid in the abdomen
  • Breathing difficulties

OHSS typically occurs at two stages:

  • Early – Within 1 to 5 days after your hCG-Ovidrel trigger.
  • Late – Within 10 to 15 days after your hCG-Ovidrel trigger as a result of pregnancy

There are varying degrees of Ovarian Hyper-stimulation:

  • Mild to Moderate: treated with outpatient management
  • Severe: treated with pain control, intravenous fluid replacement and hospitalization
    • This can result in Increased concentration of red blood cells and platelets and increase one’s chances of blood clots.
    • Kidney and liver failure
    • Death

The risk of severe OHSS is very rare affecting 0.2 percent or less of women who undergo IVF treatment. Because severe OHSS is 4 to 12 times higher if pregnancy occurs, sometimes hCG-Ovidrel trigger is withheld or embryos are frozen to reduce the possibility of this occurring.

Cancer:

Evidence does not support a relationship between fertility drugs and an increased risk of breast, ovarian, or other cancers.

Risks of Pregnancy:

Compared to naturally conceived pregnancies, IVF pregnancies are associated with increased risks of certain conditions including

  1. Pre-eclampsia-60%
  2. Placenta Previa-119%
  3. Placeta Abruption-114%
  4. Gestational Diabetes-100%
  5. C-Section 110%

Executive Summary of a National Institute of Child Health and Human Development Workshop held in September 2005, as reported in the journal Obstetrics & Gynecology, vol. 109, no. 4, pages 967-77, 2007).


Some of these risks stem may be related to the higher average age of women who conceive with IVF.


In addition, the underlying cause of infertility may also be the root cause of the increased pregnancy related complications.

Multiple Gestations:

Overall, nationally, almost 25 to 30% of IVF pregnancies are twins or higher-order multiple gestations (triplets or greater).

The risk of identical twinning also is increased and occurs in 1.5% to 4.5% of IVF pregnancies.

In general, IVF twins deliver on average three weeks earlier and weigh 1,000 grams less than singleton babies. Triplet (and greater) pregnancies deliver before 32 weeks (7 months) in up to 50% of cases.

As result, premature babies may have a prolonged stay in the neonatal intensive care unit and have a greater risk of cerebral palsy.

Abnormal Pregnancies:

Although embryos are transferred directly into the uterus with IVF, ectopic (tubal, cervical and abdominal) pregnancies are slightly increased.

Termination of these abnormal pregnancies include either medical therapy with methotrexate (a weak chemotherapy drug) or surgery to remove the pregnancy.

Alternative to IVF (putting sperm together with eggs in the laboratory)

There are several alternatives to doing IVF and an embryo transfer. While these procedures are not routinely done as compared to 15-20 years ago, they include:

  • Gamete intrafallopian transfer (GIFT) where eggs and sperm are placed directly into the fallopian tube
  • Zygote intrafallopian transfer (ZIFT) where fertilized eggs are placed directly into the fallopian tube
  • Tubal embryo transfer (TET) where developing embryos are placed directly into the fallopian tube

Gametes (sperm and/or eggs) may be frozen instead of creating embryos thus avoiding issues related to disposition of any cryopreserved embryos in the future.

Lastly, other alternatives to IVF include intrauterine inseminations, surgery if its applicable to you, or not to undergo therapy at all.

Social/Legal

Ethical and Religious Considerations in Infertility Treatment:

IVF is a unique procedure in that it involves creating human embryos outside the body.It can create a number of issues that are often overlooked including the:

  • 1. The production of excess embryos and what one should do with them.
  • 2. The creation of multiple pregnancies, for example: triplets and consideration to such issues as selective reduction.
  • 3. Standing within your religious community.

For those who desire, we encourage further discussion with our staff and perhaps considering consulting with religious leaders or ethicists for guidance these challenging situations.

Psychosocial Effects of Infertility Treatment:

A diagnosis of infertility can be a devastating and life-altering event.

Infertility and its treatment can affect you and your partner medically, financially, socially, emotionally and psychologically.

Feelings of anxiousness, depression, isolation, and helplessness are not uncommon among patients undergoing treatment.

Strained and stressful relations with partners and other loved ones are not uncommon as treatment progresses.

While it is normal to experience emotional ups and downs, it is important to recognize severe feelings.

Our health-care team and mental health professionals are available to address emotional and physical symptoms that can accompany infertility.


Legal Considerations to Offspring:

The laws regarding embryo cryopreservation, future disposition including donating to research or to other couples and parent-child status of any resulting child or children may be unsettled in the state in which an ART program is located.

We certainly do not want to give any legal advice.

However, You may want to consult a lawyer who is experienced in the area of reproductive law and embryo cryopreservation and disposition.

Reporting Outcomes:

The 1992 Fertility Clinic Success Rate and Certification Act requires the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) to collect cycle outcomes on all IVF cycles performed in the United States each year and requires us to report success rates using these data.

As such, data from your IVF procedure will be provided to the CDC, and to the Society of Assisted Reproductive Technologies (SART) of the American Society of Reproductive Medicine (ASRM).

We may be requested to secure additional information from your IVF cycle.

Your information may be used BUT is disclosed in accordance with HIPAA guidelines.

Financial:

Every patient should have a financial consult and been given a copy of benefits from the insurance cards we provided to ARI. Patients are personally financially responsible for the expense of treatment and additional expenses for medical problems or those for a child.
